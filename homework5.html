Theory/Research


Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality states that, for any pair of vectors u and v in an inner product space, the following inequality holds:

|u ⋅ v| ≤ ||u|| * ||v||

Proof:

Consider the vectors u and v in the inner product space. We define the following quadratic function in a real parameter t:

f(t) = ||u - t*v||²

Expand the expression for f(t):

f(t) = (u - t*v) ⋅ (u - t*v)
f(t) = u ⋅ u - 2t(u ⋅ v) + t²(v ⋅ v)

Since the square of a norm is always non-negative, we have:

f(t) ≥ 0

Now rewrite the quadratic function:

f(t) = ||u||² - 2t(u ⋅ v) + t²||v||²

Since f(t) ≥ 0 for all t, the discriminant of this quadratic must be non-positive. The discriminant of f(t) is:

D = (-2(u ⋅ v))² - 4 * ||v||² * ||u||²

We require the discriminant to be non-positive:

D = 4(u ⋅ v)² - 4 * ||u||² * ||v||² ≤ 0

Divide both sides by 4:

(u ⋅ v)² ≤ ||u||² * ||v||²

Finally, take the square root of both sides (noting that both sides are non-negative):

|u ⋅ v| ≤ ||u|| * ||v||
Conclusion:

We have thus proven the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

|u ⋅ v| ≤ ||u|| * ||v||


Independence vs. Uncorrelation


Independence

Two random variables, X and Y, are said to be independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability distribution of the other. In formal terms, for any events A and B:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B)

Independence implies that the joint distribution of X and Y factors into the product of their marginal distributions:

P(X, Y) = P(X) * P(Y)

In terms of expectations, if X and Y are independent, then:

E[X * Y] = E[X] * E[Y]

Independence is a stronger condition than decorrelation, as it implies that there is no dependency between the variables at all, including higher-order relationships.


Uncorrelation

Two random variables, X and Y, are said to be uncorrelated if their covariance is zero:

Cov(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X]) * (Y - E[Y])] = 0

Uncorrelation implies that there is no linear relationship between the variables. However, it does not rule out other forms of dependency, such as nonlinear relationships. This means that two variables can be uncorrelated but still dependent in a more complex way, for example, through a non-linear function.

Uncorrelation is a weaker condition than independence, as it only indicates the absence of linear dependence, but does not preclude other types of dependence.


Conceptual Differences

The key differences between independence and uncorrelation lie in their definitions and implications:

  • Independence: Implies that the occurrence of one event does not influence the occurrence of another. It covers all forms of dependency, both linear and nonlinear.
  • Uncorrelation: Implies that there is no linear relationship between the two variables. However, uncorrelated variables can still have a nonlinear dependency.

In summary, uncorrelated variables are not necessarily independent, but independent variables are always uncorrelated. Independence is a stronger condition than uncorrelation because it covers all types of relationships, while uncorrelation only addresses linear relationships.


Possible Measures

There are several ways to measure the independence or lack thereof between random variables:

  • Correlation Coefficient: This is a measure of linear dependence between two variables. If the correlation coefficient is zero, the variables are uncorrelated. However, this does not imply independence.
  • Covariance: A measure of the joint variability of two variables. A covariance of zero indicates that the variables are uncorrelated.
  • Mutual Information: A more general measure of dependence, mutual information captures both linear and nonlinear dependencies. Zero mutual information implies independence.

While correlation and covariance are useful for detecting linear relationships, mutual information is a more comprehensive measure for identifying any form of dependency between variables.


Conclusion

In conclusion, independence is a stronger concept than uncorrelation, as it implies no relationship at all between two variables, while uncorrelation only rules out linear relationships. Understanding these concepts and the measures used to quantify them is essential for analyzing statistical relationships between variables.
