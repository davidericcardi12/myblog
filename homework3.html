    Why the Median Minimizes the Sum of Absolute Deviations

    The goal is to find the value of c that minimizes the sum of absolute deviations, defined as:

    S(c) = Σ |xi - c|

    where xi are the data points. The value that minimizes this sum is the median. Here's the proof:

    1. Define the sum of absolute deviations:

    S(c) = |x1 - c| + |x2 - c| + ... + |xn - c|

    This is the sum of the absolute differences between each data point xi and the point c we're looking to minimize.

    2. Split the data:

    Arrange the data in non-decreasing order: x1 ≤ x2 ≤ ... ≤ xn. To minimize the sum, we need to consider how the sum behaves to the left and right of c. If we move c slightly left or right, it affects the deviations differently depending on whether c is less than or greater than the data points.

    3. Median minimizes the sum:

    Suppose c is the median of the dataset, which splits the data into two halves. When c is the median, the number of data points smaller than or equal to c is the same as the number of data points larger than or equal to c. Moving c in either direction will increase the absolute deviations for one half more than it decreases for the other half, resulting in a larger sum.

    Therefore, the sum of absolute deviations is minimized when c is the median.

    Conclusion

    Formally, the median minimizes the function:

    S(c) = Σ |xi - c|

    This property makes the median the optimal choice when dealing with absolute deviations.



    Defining Location Statistics

    Location statistics, often referred to as central tendency or measures of center, provide a way to summarize a distribution. Here, we explore various conceptual definitions of location statistics and their generalizations, leading to a potentially infinite set of definitions.

    1. Arithmetic Mean

    The arithmetic mean is the sum of all observations divided by the number of observations:

    μ = (Σ xi) / n

    It is the most common measure of central tendency, representing the balance point of a dataset.

    Generalization:

    By altering the weights of observations or applying different functions, we can derive various means, such as the weighted mean:

    μw = (Σ wi xi) / (Σ wi)

    2. Median

    The median is the middle value when data is sorted. It divides the dataset into two equal halves:

    For a dataset {x1, x2, ..., xn}, if n is odd, the median is:

    Median = x((n + 1) / 2)

    Generalization:

    We can extend the concept of the median to any quantile, leading to quantile-based location statistics:

    Qk = x((nk) / 100) for k = 0, 1, ..., 100

    3. Mode

    The mode is the most frequently occurring value in a dataset. It can be used for categorical data:

    For a dataset, if xi occurs most frequently, then:

    Mode = xmax where f(xi) = max(f(xj))

    Generalization:

    We can define multiple modes (bimodal, trimodal, etc.) or explore the frequency distribution to derive other measures of central tendency based on frequency thresholds.

    4. Geometric Mean

    The geometric mean is the nth root of the product of n values, suitable for multiplicative processes:

    GM = (x1 * x2 * ... * xn)1/n

    Generalization:

    By using different bases or modifications to the product, we can create other means, such as the generalized geometric mean:

    GMg = (Σ gi * xi)1/S

    5. Other Location Statistics

    Other measures include:

    • Trimean: A combination of mean and median.
    • Winsorized Mean: Mean after replacing extreme values.
    • Midrange: Average of the maximum and minimum values.

    Generalization:

    Each of these can be modified to create new statistics. For instance, adjusting the weights or applying different transformations to data points can lead to an infinite variety of measures.

    Conclusion

    Location statistics provide essential insights into data distributions. By exploring various definitions and generalizations, we can discover countless ways to summarize and understand data effectively.
