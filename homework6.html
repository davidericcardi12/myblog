Theory/Research


The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus consists of two main parts:

  1. Part 1: If a function f is continuous on the interval [a, b], then the function F(x) = ∫ax f(t) dt is continuous on [a, b], differentiable on (a, b), and its derivative is f(x), i.e., F'(x) = f(x).
  2. Part 2: If F is any antiderivative of f, i.e., F'(x) = f(x), then the definite integral of f from a to b can be computed as ∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a).
Relationship with Probability Density Function (PDF)

A Probability Density Function (PDF), denoted as f_X(x), is a function that describes the likelihood of a random variable taking a particular value. The PDF must satisfy two conditions:

  • f_X(x) ≥ 0 for all values of x (non-negative values).
  • The total area under the curve of the PDF is 1, i.e., ∫-∞∞ f_X(x) dx = 1.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus plays a significant role when working with the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF). The relationship is established as follows:

If we define the CDF of a random variable X as:

F_X(x) = P(X ≤ x) = ∫-∞x f_X(t) dt

Then the CDF F_X(x) represents the cumulative probability that X is less than or equal to x.

According to Part 1 of the FTC, the derivative of the CDF with respect to x gives the PDF:

F_X'(x) = f_X(x)

This means that the PDF is the derivative of the CDF, which is a direct application of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Demonstration

Let's demonstrate the relationship between the PDF and CDF more formally. Consider a continuous random variable X with a PDF f_X(x) and a CDF F_X(x).

The CDF is defined as:

F_X(x) = ∫-∞x f_X(t) dt

By applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) Part 1, we can differentiate the CDF:

F_X'(x) = f_X(x)

This derivative represents the rate of change of the cumulative probability, which corresponds to the probability density at that specific point x.

Application in Probability Theory

This relationship is crucial in probability theory because it allows us to switch between the PDF and CDF by differentiation or integration, making it easier to compute probabilities and expectations for continuous random variables.

For example, the probability that the random variable X lies within a specific interval is given by:

P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a)

Here, the CDF values at b and a give the cumulative probabilities, and their difference yields the probability of X being between a and b.

Summary

To summarize, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus provides a fundamental link between the Probability Density Function (PDF) and the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF). The PDF is the derivative of the CDF, and the CDF is the integral of the PDF. This relationship is essential in understanding how cumulative probabilities accumulate from individual probability densities, and vice versa.
