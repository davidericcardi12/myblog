Sampling Mean and Variance


In statistics, when we take samples from a population, we often compute the mean and variance of the sample. These are key measures that help us understand the behavior of the sample and make inferences about the population.

Sampling Mean

The sample mean is the average of the values in the sample. For a random sample \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \) of size \( n \), the sample mean is calculated as:

\[ \bar{X} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \]

The sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean \( \mu \). This means:

\[ \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}] = \mu \]

The variance of the sample mean \( \bar{X} \) is related to the population variance \( \sigma^2 \), and it decreases as the sample size increases:

\[ \text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \]

This formula shows that larger samples result in more precise estimates of the mean.

Sampling Variance

The sample variance is a measure of how spread out the values in the sample are. It is computed as:

\[ S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X})^2 \]

Here, \( n-1 \) is used instead of \( n \) to correct for bias in estimating the population variance. This is called Bessel's correction. The sample variance is an unbiased estimator of the population variance \( \sigma^2 \), meaning:

\[ \mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2 \]

Distributions

The sampling distribution of the sample mean \( \bar{X} \) has the following properties:

  • If the population is normally distributed, then \( \bar{X} \) is also normally distributed:

    • \[ \bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \]

  • If the population is not normally distributed, the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that for large \( n \), the distribution of \( \bar{X} \) approximates a normal distribution.

The sampling distribution of the sample variance \( S^2 \) is related to the chi-squared distribution:

\[ \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1} \]

This means that the sample variance is not normally distributed but instead follows a chi-squared distribution with \( n-1 \) degrees of freedom.

Main Features
  • The sample mean \( \bar{X} \) is more accurate as the sample size increases, due to the reduction in variance.
  • The sample variance \( S^2 \) provides an unbiased estimate of the population variance.
  • Both statistics depend on the sample size and the variability of the population.

Lebesgue–Stieltjes Integration and Its Applications

The Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral is a generalization of the regular Lebesgue integral. It allows integration with respect to a function, which can be any real-valued function, not just a simple measure. This makes it very useful in various fields such as Probability Theory and Measure Theory.

Definition

The general form of the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral is:

        ∫_a^b f(x) dg(x)

Here, f(x) is a function to integrate and g(x) is a function that defines the measure with respect to which we are integrating. The function g(x) is typically of bounded variation, which means its increase is controlled over the interval [a, b].

Mathematical Representation

If g(x) is a continuous, non-decreasing function, the integral can be written as:

        ∫_a^b f(x) dg(x) = lim (Δx → 0) Σ f(x_i) (g(x_i+1) - g(x_i))

In this formula, f(x_i) is the value of the function at some sample points, and Δx is the partitioning of the interval into small subintervals. The sum represents the Riemann-Stieltjes sum, which approximates the integral.

Applications to Probability Theory

In Probability Theory, the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral plays an important role in defining expected values and other statistical measures. For example, if F(x) is a cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a random variable, the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral can be used to compute expected values:

        E[X] = ∫_a^b x dF(x)

Here, E[X] is the expected value of the random variable X, and F(x) is the CDF of X.

Applications to Measure Theory

In Measure Theory, the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral is a tool for integrating with respect to measures that are not necessarily simple. For example, if g(x) represents a probability measure, we can compute the integral of a function over the measure space:

        ∫_X f(x) dμ(x)

Where μ(x) is a measure, and f(x) is a measurable function. The Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral generalizes the concept of integrating over a space, providing more flexibility in handling complex spaces.
